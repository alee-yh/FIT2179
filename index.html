Does Money Buy
Happiness in Malaysia?

Explore how income, geography, and identity shape well-being.

Malaysians today earn more than ever. Yet happiness is not distributed equally. Some states report strong well-being despite modest incomes, while wealthier regions do not always top rankings.

This visualisation follows the data through three questions: how happy are Malaysians, does more money help, and what else matters? The answers are more complicated than income alone can explain.

Part I How Happy Are Malaysians?

What Shapes Happiness in Malaysia?

The Malaysian Happiness Index (MHI) measures well-being across 13 domains. Religion and Family score highest. Income and Culture sit well below the average.

Blue dots: Score less than average
Yellow dots: Score greater than average

Not Every State Shares
the Same Profile

Terengganu scores well across almost every domain. W.P. Labuan and Pulau Pinang trail behind consistently, even in domains unrelated to income.

Darker = Higher score.

The Happiest States are NOT the Richest

Terengganu leads with 8.64. The wealthiest federal territories cluster near the bottom of the ranking.

Part II Does More Money Make Malaysians Happier?

Income and Happiness
Don't Always Align

Each dot is a state. The median split lines divide it into four quadrants.

Terengganu records a median household income above the national median while also achieving a happiness index substantially higher than the national median.

In contrast, Selangor and some federal territories exhibit considerably higher median household incomes but report happiness scores below the national median.

Satisfaction Doesn't
Follow Income

Terengganu scores the highest on income satisfaction despite modest earnings.

W.P. Putrajaya, Malaysia's highest-income state, falls below the national average of 6.40.

Expectations, not earnings, may drive how content people feel.

Incomes Grew, But Unevenly

Between 2019 and 2024, Selangor surged 31%.
W.P. Kuala Lumpur grew just 2.3%, suggesting diminishing returns at the top of the income ladder.

Part III Happiness is about more than money

Women are Happier
in Most States

Perak shows the widest gap (+0.12 for women). Sabah is one of the few states where men score higher.

Select a state to highlight it.

Ethnic Groups Share
Similar Totals

But, the domain breakdown differs. The Others group scores notably lower on Income and Culture.

All groups score high on Family and Religion.

Where you Live Shapes
What Makes you Happy

Urban residents score higher on Communication (+0.69) and Education.

Rural residents lead on Social Participation (-0.42) and Time Use.

Overall happiness is almost identical: 7.61 urban vs. 7.56 rural.

Indicating a good balance of well-being levels across different strata.

Happiness Shifts Across Age Groups

Time Use satisfaction dips but recovers after 55.

Income satisfaction falls steadily from 55 onward.

Darker = Higher score.

The Verdict

So, Can Money
Buy Happiness?

Income improves comfort and opportunity. States with growing wages show modest well-being gains over time.

The happiest state in 2024 is not the richest. The widest inequality gaps align with the lowest happiness scores.

Happiness in Malaysia is built from family, faith, community, and time. Money supports these things, but it cannot replace them.
